The wooden door is cold. Older than cold. It absorbs the warmth from his palm before he can register the contact, and Karim pulls back without meaning to. His breath makes small clouds in the April air that shouldn't be this sharp, this crystalline. Midnight doesn't usually feel like this—like the world has stopped exhaling.
The brass handle catches light from somewhere. Not from the bar. From a source that exists behind his eyes.
He pushes.
The hinges don't creak. They whisper. A sound like paper sliding across glass, or fingernails on silk. The smell hits him first—not the stale beer and wood smoke from his first visit. This is something older. Cardamom. Sandalwood. And underneath it, something burnt. Not tobacco. Something ceremonial. Something used.
The interior has changed.
The bar is still there—the same mahogany counter, the same brass fixtures catching light that has no source—but the space around it has expanded. Impossible. The narrow room he remembered from March is now cathedral-like, though the walls remain unchanged. The wood-paneled walls press in and recede simultaneously, and his eyes can't reconcile the distance. The floor beneath his shoes is stone now, not wood. Worn stone. The kind that thousands of feet have crossed.
Mahi stands behind the counter. Same position as before. Same immaculate waistcoat, same antique collar. But his face is different. Not in features—Karim can't remember the features clearly enough to notice a change—but in presence. There is something behind his eyes that wasn't there in March. Something that has been waiting.
"You came back," Mahi says.
Not a question. A confirmation of fact. The voice is the same—cultured, measured—but underneath it, Karim hears layers. Multiple voices occupying the same throat. Or perhaps his hearing has fractured.
"I said I would," Karim answers.
The words sound absurd. A month of ritual, a month of surrendering reason to the city's logic, and now he speaks as if this is a business appointment. As if he's kept his word with a tailor.
"Did you?" Mahi tilts his head. The movement is too smooth. Vertebrae shouldn't move that way. "Time is a curious thing, Karim. To you, a month has passed. To the city, a single night. To me—" He pauses. The smile is not unkind, but it contains something that predates kindness. "To me, it is the same April 30th it was last year. And the year before."
Karim's hands begin to sweat.
"How many people have made this bargain?" The question escapes before he can stop it.
Mahi's smile deepens. He moves toward the shelves behind him—not walking, but sliding, as if the intervening space has folded. His fingers select a bottle from the rows. It is old. The glass is the color of dried blood. The label is written in script Karim doesn't recognize, though he understands it completely: Rna (Debt).
"Does the number matter?" Mahi asks. "The Vedas teach that karma is not punishment, Karim. It is debt. You owe the universe a death. Your wife and child owed it a life. Some debts, when paid, create new debts. Others—" He sets the bottle on the counter with a sound like a bone settling into earth. "Others allow for adjustment."
Karim's mind lurches. In his chest, something like recognition, or like dread wearing recognition's face.
"You can't undo what you did," Mahi continues, pouring clear liquid into a glass. The pour is longer than the bottle should allow. "The Fidi Abode doesn't offer forgetting. It offers renegotiation. Your wife left you because you chose the bottle. Your child grew up without a father because you chose the bottle. That is not negotiable. That debt is paid—written into the cellular structure of both of them."
The glass sits on the counter. Karim hasn't moved.
"But," Mahi says, "you might choose differently. In this timeline. In this iteration."
The air cracks. Not metaphorically. A actual crack propagates through the space like a fissure through glass, and through it, Karim sees—something else. Another bar. Another Karim, sitting at a different table, head in his hands. The version in that space looks up, and their eyes meet across the fracture, and Karim sees himself from the outside: gaunt, aged, skin the color of spoiled paper.
Then the crack closes.
"There are many Karims," Mahi says softly. "Most of them made different choices at different points. This is the function of the Fidi Abode. Not redemption. Redistribution. Your karma doesn't disappear. It is moved. Laterally. Into possibility spaces where it can take different form."
Karim's throat is dry.
"What's the price?" His voice doesn't sound like his own. It sounds like something speaking through him, using him as a resonance chamber. "What do you want from me?"
Mahi gestures toward the glass.
"Sit."
Karim doesn't remember walking to the chair. He's sitting now. The seat is carved wood, older than the bar, older than the building. His hands rest on the table's surface and he can feel grooves beneath his palms—initials, perhaps, or sigils. Names of other people who have sat here, at this exact moment, on April 30th at midnight.
He doesn't want to ask, but his mouth opens anyway.
"Is this—am I—"
"Yes," Mahi says. "You are still in the city. You are also still in the bar. Time here operates on a different principle. To the people sleeping in the streets above us, a second has passed. To you, it will be as long as it needs to be."
Above us. The words suggest depth. They are not in the bar. They are beneath it.
"I don't understand," Karim says.
"No," Mahi agrees. "But you will. This is the function of the contract. Not to make you understand, but to make you complicit. There is a difference."
The glass remains on the table. Karim stares into it. The liquid is colorless and still. It reflects nothing.
"What are you?" Karim asks.
Mahi sets down a leather folder. It is the color of dried skin. The moment Karim sees it, he knows it is old—not decades old, but centuries. Perhaps longer. The leather still holds the shape of the thing it was flayed from.
"I am what happens when someone refuses their debt," Mahi says. "My father was a king, Karim. A man who believed that his station absolved him from karma. He refused to pay his debts. So he was removed. Banished. Unmade. I am what remains when a consciousness is extracted from the cycle of rebirth and forced to exist between iterations. Neither living nor dead. Neither bound nor free. I exist in the thresholds. And I have learned, over many April 30ths, that there are others like you—people whose debts have calcified. People whose refusal to pay has hardened into their bones. They come here, believing they can negotiate away their suffering. And they can."
He opens the folder.
The contract is not written on paper. The text is carved directly into a membrane—thin, translucent, vaguely organic. Karim can see through it, and what he sees beyond the words is not the wall. It is something moving. Something that breathes.
"What do I have to give?" Karim's voice is steady now. He doesn't know why. Fear should be breaking him open, but instead, he feels a terrible clarity. The kind clarity that comes only to those who have already died, in every way that matters.
"The heaviest thing you carry," Mahi says, and his voice now contains all the voices it has ever contained—a chorus of the dispossessed. "Your guilt will take the form of a weight. Not metaphorical. You will carry it on your shoulders for the rest of your life. It will not diminish. It will not forgive you. Each morning, it will be there. Each night, it will press you deeper into the earth. In exchange, Manat will not remember leaving you. Tapur will not remember your absence. They will live a life in which you chose differently. They will sleep without the shadow of your addiction splitting their dreams."
"And me?" Karim is whispering.
"You will know," Mahi says. "You will carry the knowledge of who you were, in every iteration except this one. You will have the memory of the bottle, and the memory of their departure, and the memory of the month you spent searching for this bar. But you will wake, every morning, in a bed that has never been slept in by a ghost. You will see their faces without the distortion of your failure. And you will never touch them without feeling, beneath your hands, the weight that says: this is not real. This is the price."
Karim reaches for the glass.
His hand stops.
In the glass, he sees not his reflection, but his daughter's face. Tapur. Seven years old in this image, though he has not seen her face clearly in two years. She is smiling. Behind her, Manat, also smiling. They are in a room he doesn't recognize, in a life he hasn't lived, and they are looking at something beyond the glass. Something he can never reach.
"Will I be happy?" he asks.
Mahi's expression doesn't change.
"No. But they will be. In the accounting of karma, that is a favorable exchange. You will have suffering. They will have peace. The universe demands balance. The Fidi Abode merely provides the mechanism."
Karim's hand moves without his permission.
He lifts the glass. The liquid is warm. It tastes like nothing—or like everything, all flavors existing simultaneously and canceling each other into neutrality. As it slides down his throat, he feels something leave him. Not his memories. Not his guilt. Something older. Something that has been sitting in his chest since childhood, some essential weight that kept him anchored to the person he was.
The glass empties.
"Read the contract," Mahi says.
Karim's eyes fall on the words carved into the membrane. He cannot read them—they are not in any language he recognizes—but he understands them completely. The meaning does not travel through his eyes. It replaces him. He is the contract now. He is the debt, and the payment, and the space between them.
His hand reaches for the writing instrument Mahi has placed beside the fold.
The pen is not pen-shaped. It is smooth. Warm. It pulses slightly in his grip, as if it has a heartbeat.
He signs.
The name that appears is not his own. It is written in script that moves, that rewrites itself, that becomes other names superimposed over his own. Karim. Karam. Karun. Karma. All the same signature, written by hands that are not his hands, in an April 30th that is all April 30ths.
The membrane absorbs the signature. It darkens where his name appears. The darkness spreads like ink in water, like blood in milk, and Karim feels it spreading through him as well. Through his veins. Through the chambers of his heart. Through the synapses where memory lives.
He closes his eyes.
When he opens them, he is on a street he recognizes. Dawn light is breaking over the buildings. The bar has vanished. His hands are empty. His mind is clear in a way it hasn't been in years, but it is also—altered. There are two streams running through his consciousness now. One remembers the Fidi Abode. One remembers a different April 30th, one where he never found the bar at all. Both are true. Both are him.
He walks. His legs know the way.
The apartment building is unchanged. The stairs are the same stairs. His key works the same lock. He opens the door and steps into a life he both remembers and doesn't remember living.
Manat is in the kitchen. She is making tea. She turns at the sound of his entry and her face opens into something like relief, something like morning.
"You're back early," she says. "I thought you were working the late shift."
Karim doesn't remember a shift. He doesn't remember working. But the words she speaks land in a space in his mind that is prepared to receive them. There is a job. There is a routine. There is an entire month he has lived without remembering.
"Traffic was clear," he hears himself say.
She pours two cups. The steam rises between them, and for a moment, Karim sees through it—a flash of the bar's interior, a glimpse of Mahi's face, the weight descending onto his shoulders with the heaviness of a stone angel.
Then Manat hands him the tea, and the vision breaks.
"Tapur is still asleep," she says. "She had a nightmare. Something about a man made of doors."
Karim's cup stops halfway to his lips.
"What kind of man?" he asks.
Manat's expression shifts. A flicker of confusion crosses her face—not from the question, but from something deeper. As if she is remembering something she knows she didn't experience.
"I don't know," she says. "She wouldn't explain. But when I held her, she kept saying the same thing. Over and over."
"What did she say?"
"That she wanted to apologize to someone. For forgetting him."
The tea is scalding against his tongue. Karim doesn't stop drinking. Each sip burns, and in the burning, he feels the weight settling more firmly onto his shoulders. It is not metaphorical anymore. It is as real as the cup in his hands. As real as the woman before him who is his wife, in this iteration, in this world where his addiction never happened, where his daughter grew up with both her parents, where the three of them are whole.
As real as the knowledge that somewhere, in some other April 30th, a different Karim is still wandering the streets. Still looking for the bar.
Still believing he can undo the past.
"The tea is getting cold," Manat says. "Drink it."
He drinks.
Outside the window, the city is waking. The sun is rising on a world where Karim is a different man than he was yesterday. Improved. Redeemed. Whole.
The weight on his shoulders grows heavier with each breath.
In his daughter's room, Tapur stirs in her sleep. She doesn't dream of the man made of doors anymore. Her dreams are gentle now, uncomplicated. She dreams of her father's face, clear and unmarred by the shadow of a bottle. She dreams of a life without that ghost.
She will never know what was purchased to make her dream possible.
Karim sets down the tea and listens to her breathing through the thin walls. He adds this sound to the two streams of consciousness now running through him—the life he lived, and the life he is living. Both real. Both impossible. Both the price and the payment.
The weight settles. It will never lift.
In the distance, midnight is already striking in some other iteration. In some other April 30th. In some other bar at the end of a quiet pathway, another desperate man is pushing through wooden doors, hearing the whisper of hinges, smelling burnt offerings and old regrets.
Mahi is waiting.
There are always more debts to redistribute.
