Karim had been roaming aimlessly in the streets for four hours when the bar at the end of
the alley captured his attention. He suddenly felt tired and thirsty and the overwhelming urge
to sit down to rest his legs and have a drink, although he had sat down at the park bench
and had coffee just an hour ago. As he pondered what to do, he found himself standing right
in front of the bar. He didn’t remember walking all the way down the alley to the bar, but
maybe he just had too much going on inside his head. As he pushed the wooden double
doors to enter, the bells chimed to alert the bartender. The bartender was sitting on a stool
behind the counter, reading an old leatherbound book, and swiftly rose up to greet his new
customer.

“Welcome, sir. Please take a seat,” the bartender bowed and said with an easy smile on his
lips, and pointed to the chair in front of the counter.
Karim pulled the chair and dropped his weight on it. He studied the bartender with a quick
glance. The bartender was wearing a white shirt with rolled-up sleeves, and a black
waistcoat and black bowtie. The bartender had a pixie haircut and not the slightest trace of
facial hair, and silver earrings and a nosestud adorned his face. It was hard to tell whether...
“I’m Mahi. How may I serve you tonight?” the bartender asked with that same smile. There
was something disarming about that smile.
“Actually...” Karim stuttered. “I was just passing by and just wanted to stop here for a while.”
“That’s alright. Would you like to have a drink?” Mahi asked as he started to clean a highball
glass.
Karim paused for a brief moment before answering. “Umm, no. Alcohol has caused me a lot
of problems in my life already,” Karim said, avoiding eye contact with Mahi. He let out a deep
weary sigh.
“You look like you have been through a lot,” Mahi finished cleaning the glass and picked up
another.
“You bet. I wish I had done things differently,” Karim replied, shaking his head.
“May I insist you have a drink? It’s on the house, sir,” Mahi asked politely.
Before Karim could open his mouth to protest, Mahi continued “It’s non-alcoholic. And it’s my
special,” he smiled and winked. Karim nodded his head.
Mahi stooped below the counter to get something. Karim’s attention fell on the leatherbound
book on the table. It seemed like it was from centuries ago. And there was a pocket watch
on the table. Who uses a pocket watch these days?
Mahi appeared behind the counter with a decanter.
“You don’t see people dressed like this anymore,” Karim remarked.

“Yeah. I don’t like the sign of the times,” Mahi poured the drink in one glass. “Or the place.”
He pushed the glass to Karim. “I just like to sing my own tune.”
Karim raised the glass and sniffed it. It was an odorless colorless liquid. He looked at the
bartender with curiosity.
“Don’t worry, sir. I have nothing to gain by poisoning you,” Mahi laughed and raised his
hands in surrender, then poured another drink and raised the glass.
“Cheers, sir. May we always find more than we lose!” Mahi said. They clinked their glasses
and Mahi took a long swig. Relieved, Karim took a sip too.
Instantly, he felt something different. His mind felt blank and carefree and his body felt
lighter. The lights in the room began to distort in strange ways. He looked at the glass in his
hands again.
The bar’s ambience. The antique book. The pocket watch. The bartender’s attire. It all
looked suspicious to him now.
“What is this place? Who are you?” he asked Mahi as he pushed the glass away.
“The real question, sir, is not who I am, but who you will be if you were given a second
chance.”
