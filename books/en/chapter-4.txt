29th of April.
Karim was getting ready to go out.
Just a few more hours remained before the hands of the clock crossed midnight and the calendar shed the date of April 29th in favour of April 30th.
He had woken up very early that morning. Although perhaps "woken up" was not the right phrase. Sleep had become such a rare privilege that he could hardly remember the last time he had truly rested. The hours of darkness had long since become an endless stretch of half-sleep, anxious thoughts, and restless tossing and turning.
He thought of the meeting again.
Looking back, he could not remember exactly how he had left the bar that night.
He remembered the bells above the door.
The strange drink.
The bartender's smile.
Most of all, he remembered the promise.
A second chance.
The idea should have been absurd. Instead, it lingered. Long after the effects of the drink had faded and the memory of the bar had begun to blur, that promise remained—a fading ember that refused to die.
Karim often wondered why he had not asked more questions that day. Why had he accepted the words of a complete stranger so readily? Why had he not demanded clear answers?
Yet whenever he tried to examine that night logically, his thoughts drifted back to Mahi.
There had been something about the enigmatic bartender. A peculiar charisma that seemed to bypass reason itself. A strange certainty hidden beneath his smile.
By the time Karim's thoughts had cleared, he was already halfway through his journey home.
He went back the very next day.
The bar was gone.
At first, he thought he had simply forgotten the way. He searched the neighbourhood again and again. He asked around. He retraced every road he could remember.
Nothing.
No wooden double doors.
No bells.
No warm amber lights.
No bartender waiting behind the counter with an ancient book and a pocket watch.
It was as if the entire encounter had never happened.
Eventually Karim returned to the apartment he rented. Although, calling it a home would have been an insult to the word itself.
The one-bedroom flat was devoid of warmth or human presence. Clothes lay scattered across the floor. Unwashed dishes occupied the kitchen sink. Empty spaces lingered where laughter and life had once existed.
Grief had long since made itself a permanent resident there.
The days that followed moved painfully slowly.
Again and again, Karim replayed the conversation in his mind.
Who—or what—had he met that night?
Some days he wondered whether he had encountered the Devil himself, a being offering second chances in exchange for a hidden price too terrible to comprehend.
Other days he remembered the stories his grandfather used to tell him as a child—tales of mysterious beings who granted wishes to desperate people and altered the course of their lives forever.
And sometimes he considered an even stranger possibility.
That Mahi was neither of those things.
That he was something else entirely.
Something older.
Something the world had given different names throughout history in a futile attempt to understand.
What troubled Karim most was that he could never find a satisfactory answer.
Only one fact remained certain.
Looking back through his messages and receipts, he eventually realized that he had met Mahi on the twentieth of March.
The day of the spring equinox.
A day when light and darkness stood in perfect balance.
Karim had always taken pride in being a man of logic.
Superstition, miracles, destiny—he had spent most of his life dismissing such notions as comforting stories people told themselves when reality became too difficult to bear.
But logic was a luxury for those who still had something left to lose.
A drowning man does not stop to question the hand reaching out to him.
He grabs hold, tightly.
By the time Karim met Mahi, there was very little left in his life worth looking forward to. If a stranger, a devil, a genie, or something even more unfathomable offered him a chance to undo his mistakes, he knew he would take it without hesitation.
The pain of not being able to speak to Manat remained unbearable.
The inability to reach out to Tapur hurt even more.
There was a constant ache lodged somewhere behind his ribs, a heaviness in his chest that never seemed to leave. It accompanied him everywhere—from the moment he woke up until exhaustion finally forced his eyes shut.
His thoughts became trapped in an endless cycle.
One moment he wondered about Mahi and the impossible bar that appeared and disappeared without explanation.
The next he imagined what form that second chance might take.
Then the memories would return.
The first day he met Manat.
The first conversation they shared.
The day Tapur entered the world.
The years when it felt like the three of them against everyone else.
The years when life still seemed kind.
And the day he lost it all.
Those memories replayed themselves endlessly.
With each repetition, the sense of loss became sharper.
The emptiness in his chest deepened.
A dull ache lingered at the back of his head, robbing him of sleep and following him through every waking hour.
Yet alongside the pain existed hope.
A second chance.
Fragile.
Irrational.
Perhaps even foolish.
Still, Karim clung to it.
Whenever the urge to drink returned, he found himself returning to that promise. He imagined speaking to Manat again. Hearing Tapur's laughter. Standing before them without shame.
The details never remained the same.
The longing always did.
Hope.
Regret.
Memory.
Loss.
Again, and again and then again.
Sometimes Karim wondered if memories could become an addiction of their own.
Stronger than alcohol.
Far more difficult to escape.
Over the past month, Karim had developed a routine of sorts.
Every evening, when the last rays of sunlight disappeared beneath the horizon and darkness slowly reclaimed the world, he would leave his apartment.
At first, he had not realized what he was doing.
Only later did he understand that he had been trying to recreate that night.
He tried to remember the exact hour he had left home. The exact roads he had taken. The exact neighbourhoods he had wandered through before finding himself standing in front of the mysterious bar.
It became a ritual.
He would leave at roughly the same time.
Walk through roughly the same streets.
Roam through the same districts.
Then, after reaching the area where he believed he had met Mahi, he would allow himself to wander aimlessly, hoping that somehow, by chance or fate, he would stumble upon the place again.
Deep down, Karim knew how irrational it was.
The bar had vanished.
No amount of walking had brought it back.
Yet he continued.
A drowning man does not abandon a piece of driftwood simply because it cannot carry his weight.
He clings to it anyway.
Part of him hoped he would meet the bartender before the appointed day.
Part of him hoped he could somehow accelerate whatever miracle had been promised to him.
Perhaps he could undo his mistakes sooner.
Perhaps he could return to the days when Tapur's laughter echoed through the house.
Perhaps he could return to the days when life still felt alive.
And so Karim wandered.
Through familiar streets.
Through forgotten corners of the city.
Through memories.
And now, finally, it was the twenty-ninth of April.
For the first time, Karim was afraid.
Afraid that none of it had been real.
Afraid that the encounter had been nothing more than a hallucination born from grief, alcohol, and loneliness.
Afraid that he had spent an entire month chasing a fantasy.
Yet as evening fell, he performed the ritual once more.
He left his apartment.
Walked the familiar route.
Passed the same intersections.
The same buildings.
The same streets.
Only this time his heart beat faster than ever before.
Anxiety wrapped itself around his chest like a tightening vise.
For a moment he struggled to breathe.
The world seemed to narrow around him.
His vision blurred.
He stopped.
Closed his eyes.
And forced himself to take slow, deliberate breaths.
In.
Out.
In.
Out.
Gradually the panic loosened its grip.
Karim continued walking.
This time he stopped trying to guide himself.
Instead, he surrendered to the city.
He allowed the noise around him to carry him forward.
The endless traffic.
The distant conversations.
The constant hum of human life.
Slowly the busy streets gave way to quieter roads.
Concrete yielded to patches of green.
Here and there, trees interrupted the city's relentless expansion.
The crowds thinned.
Streetlights became less frequent.
The air felt calmer.
Karim kept walking.
Without purpose.
Without checking the time.
Without realizing how far he had come.
Minutes blended into hours.
The city grew quieter.
The night grew deeper.
And somewhere in the distance, a clock began to chime.
Once.
Twice.
Twelve times.
Midnight.
The thirtieth of April.
Almost instinctively, Karim looked up.
At the end of a narrow pathway, illuminated by a warm and familiar glow, stood a pair of wooden double doors.
For a moment, his breath caught in his throat.
The bells.
The dim lights.
The unmistakable silhouette.
It was exactly as he remembered.
The bar had returned.
