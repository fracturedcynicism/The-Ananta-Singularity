Even now, when Karim closes his eyes, the first day he saw Manat floats back to his mind like a vivid picture.
That day, the suburbs were being washed away by torrential rain. After somehow changing buses and enduring a line of rejections from auto-rickshaw drivers, Karim reached school quite late, walking the rest of the way. A cheap umbrella hadn't been very effective at stopping the rain; his uniform was soaked. Wiping the droplets of rain off his rimless glasses, Karim saw Reshmi Ma'am entering the classroom of their eleventh-grade science section to take the first period.
“Ma'am, may I come in?” Karim stood at the classroom door with his soaked backpack.
Although all the teachers in the school liked this brilliant boy—who was good at studies but had a plain, worn-out appearance—the seemingly stern Reshmi Ma'am held a special affection for him. Karim had been studying at this school since he was young; not only did he cover his own educational expenses through various scholarships, but this gifted boy also brought prizes to the school by representing them on various stages year after year.
“Come in.”
Upon entering, Karim was momentarily stunned. Sitting at his seat, facing the window, was a girl with a hairband and a cascade of flowing hair. He hadn't seen her before; was she a new admission?
“Students, your first class test will be next to next week. Write down the chapters which have been selected for the test...” After pointing out the important sums of Sets, Chords, and Logs, the teacher paused. “Manat, Karim is the class topper; collect the notes for the upcoming exams from him.”
“Hello, will you give me your notes?” As soon as the class ended, Manat approached Karim with small, hesitant steps.
The curly hair on Manat's forehead defied the discipline of the hairband, her eyes were brown and deer-like, and a light scent of lavender wafted from her. It enveloped Karim in a kind of joy, similar to a naive little boy finding his favorite lost kite.
“Excuse me?”
“Yes, sorry,” regaining his senses, Karim handed over the notebook. With a sweet smile, Manat took the book and said as she left, “Thanks, Handuram!”
“Huh?”
Manat. Even today, when he thinks of her, the diver in Karim's chest stirs, his heart churning as it searches for a seashell. Manat was the only one who meant everything in Karim's life. A selfish father, a mentally absent mother, and the suffocating environment of his home had continuously bred a distaste for life within him. He had no friends; books were the only palette of colors in his grey existence.
Manat's arrival was like a burst of light, a storm of fresh air in that closed-off life.
This fragmented, meaningless life might have simply dwindled away if Manat hadn't come. For in everyone's ignorance, Manat had nursed the nearly dying Karim back to health with friendship, love, and attention.
A tear rolled down from Karim's eye. It has been nearly two years since Manat left him and took their Tapur with her. The voices that once breathed life into the house, the two people who had kept him alive and taught him how to dream—today, those very people have left him.
That is why, these days, Karim no longer feels like returning home.
After Manat left with Tapur, Karim would linger in the house like a haunted soul, clinging to Manat's clothes and touching Tapur's baby cot. Gradually, that empty house and the burden of unbearable memories were consuming him from the inside. Perhaps one day Karim would have committed that ultimate act, had he not come across that letter Manat wrote him during their college days.
“I love you as the plant that never blooms but carries in itself the light of hidden flowers; thanks to your love a certain solid fragrance, risen from the earth, lives darkly in my body.
I love you without knowing how, or when, or from where. I love you straightforwardly, without complexities or pride; so I love you because I know no other way
than this: where I does not exist, nor you, so close that your hand on my chest is my hand, so close that your eyes close as I fall asleep.”
Manat was very fond of Neruda; she would often tuck letters into the gaps of Karim's bag or wallet. Sometimes they were song lyrics, sometimes poems, and sometimes excerpts from a story. All those writings that teach one to dream, enlighten the mind, and teach one to patiently choose good over evil—Manat would write them all for Karim. Strangely, Manat wanted to shield Karim with all the light in the world, as if she wanted to ensure that no darkness could ever touch him; she did so much for him.
After college, upon getting jobs during campus placements, they started a home together. Bit by bit, they built their life. Exactly as Karim had dreamed: a home of joy, laughter, and happiness. A person of his own. An eternal sanctuary.
Manat's entire world revolved around Karim; that is why she had left everything behind to come to him. She always told him, “Just as Pablo Neruda loved the life inside a plant, I love you simply for the joy that you exist. That you will be in my life forever is my achievement; I have never wanted anything more than this. Since the first day I saw you in school, you have merged into my entire existence. I only want to spend a life with you. Will you stay with me?”
A deep sigh escapes from Karim's breaking chest. Why did Manat leave? How could she? For how long has he not smelled the baby-scent of Tapur? How long has it been since Manat stroked his hair?
Will life ever give him a second chance? Will his Manat and Tapur return? Will this empty house be filled with light and laughter again?
Perhaps that hope still remains. Wiping his eyes, Karim prepares himself to face Mahir.
